Quantified Dialogic Discourse in Dostoevsky

Produced by Kiun Hwang and Emma Schwarz (University of Pittsburgh)

First published in 1846, Fyodor Dostoevsky's The Double [Двойник] chronicles the mind of Yakov Petrovich Golyadkin—a Petersburg titular councillor—as he is confronted by a living mirror image of himself; moreover, this mirror image possesses all the qualities Golyadkin deems lacking in himself. The original Golyadkin is referred to as Golyadkin Senior [Голядкин-старший], while his double is referenced as Golyadkin Junior [Голядкин-младший]. Where Golyadkin Senior lacks confidence and performs poorly in social situations, Golyadkin Junior is charming and quickly outperforms his Senior at their place of work. The appearance of the titular double occurs following a social experience so humiliating, Golyadkin states that "Mr. Golyadkin was killed—killed entirely, in the full sense of the word" [«Господин Голядкин был убит—убит вполне, в полном смысле слова»]: in this rejection of the self, Golyadkin's consciousness manifests an alternate, superior version of the self, Golyadkin Junior. By the end of the story, Golyadkin's self-consciousness shatters further, and he begins to feel surrounded by other manifestations of himself; the story ends with his journey to an asylum.

The project explores Golyadkin’s self-consciousness through quantification of his inner and external dialogue, utilizing Mikhail Bakhtin’s theoretical text Problems of Dostoevsky’s Poetics as a basis for the analysis. In this work, Bakhtin introduces ideas of dialogism and polyphony to literary criticism, asserting that Dostoevsky innovated a type of polyphonic novel that is characterized by dialogic speech acts made by recognizably different voices of different characters, each of whom are fully equal and thus not subordinate to the authorial or narratorial voice. This dialogic discourse allows a plurality of consciousness and affirms multiple points of worldview. Bakhtin posits that there are three forms of speech: direct, indirect, and free indirect, as well as three forms of emotional valence in Golyadkin’s speech acts: timid, self-confident, and mocking.

To quantify this dialogic discourse, an XML encoding of the text in the original Russian was produced, tagging all instances of speech, as well as their attributes (direct vs. indirect, timid vs. self-confident, et cetera); moreover, to formalize the encoding, RelaxNG was used to write a schema, ensuring that the encoding is not only well-formed, but also adheres to the specifications of our analysis. Regular expressions were used to clean the text and to locate grammatical indications of speech (angled quotation marks or dashes). Following the location of speech acts, each act's attributes were defined. We then counted how often each character (Golyadkin, Golyadkin's double, the narrator) engages in the three types of speech, as well as how Golyadkin's speech acts shift in emotional valence over the course of the work.

Through our research, we would like to re-examine and develop Bakhtin’s literary analysis of Dostoevsky’s works, with an emphasis on self-consciousness or self-reflexiveness of the characters, beginning with The Double. For further research, the project would develop in two different directions: analyses of Gogol’s works and their influence on The Double, or analyses of Dostoevsky’s further development of dialogism and the theme of the double in his later novels, such as The Possessed and The Karamazov Brothers.
